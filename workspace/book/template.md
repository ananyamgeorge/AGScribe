# Chapter template

Target: 1200–2000 words. Shorter than you think. A chapter is done when the outcome is delivered, not when it hits a word count.

One rule: every chapter produces a concrete shippable thing the reader can use. A working skill, a checklist, a recipe, a finished loaf. If the reader can't do something by the end, the chapter isn't a chapter yet.

## Before you write a chapter

Write down one sentence: "By the end of this chapter, the reader can ________."

If you can't fill in the blank with a specific, demonstrable action, stop. The chapter isn't ready. Go back to the plan.

## Structure

### The promise (100–150 words)

Open with exactly what the reader will have at the end. Not teased. Stated.

Example: "By the time you finish this chapter, you'll have a loaf of bread cooling on your counter. Four ingredients, four hours, no kneading, no sourdough starter."

Then one short sentence on why this particular outcome matters right now. No story, no metaphor, no uncomfortable truth. The reader came for the outcome; give them the outcome first.

### The minimum necessary context (150–300 words)

Only the theory the reader needs to do the work in this specific chapter. No history, no comparisons, no "here's why this is important philosophically." If a concept can be explained in a sentence, explain it in a sentence and move on.

When a new tool or ingredient or concept appears for the first time, name it, translate it in one line, and keep going. Don't explain it twice.

### The steps (600–1000 words)

The actual work. Numbered, in order, small enough that no single step takes more than a few minutes.

Each step has:
- What the reader types or does
- What they should see
- What to do if they see something else

Show the real commands, the real measurements, the real output. No prose paragraphs explaining what a step "will accomplish." The step is the accomplishment.

### The gotcha (100–200 words)

The single thing most readers will get stuck on. Name it. Name the fix. Move on. Every chapter has one, usually two. Don't stretch to three; if there isn't a real gotcha, skip this section.

### Confirm it worked (50–100 words)

A test the reader can run to prove the thing actually works. "Your loaf should sound hollow when you tap the bottom. If it doesn't, see the gotcha section." If the test fails, point them to troubleshooting.

This section is not optional. Without it, the reader can't know if they succeeded.

### What's next (50–100 words)

One sentence on what the reader can now do that they couldn't do before. One sentence on the next chapter (only if it builds on this one).

No "leave a thread hanging." No ambiguity. The reader finishes the chapter with a working thing and a clear next step.

## Before marking a chapter complete

Outcome checks (non-negotiable):

- [ ] The one-sentence promise from the top of this template is filled in
- [ ] A reader who followed the steps would have the thing working
- [ ] The "confirm it worked" test is specific and runnable
- [ ] Every instruction in the chapter has been run by the author and verified to work
- [ ] Every claim has been fact-checked

Usefulness checks:

- [ ] You can name one specific reader who needed this chapter
- [ ] The chapter doesn't depend on a previous chapter the reader might have skipped, OR it says at the top which chapter they need to have done first
- [ ] No theory is present that isn't directly needed to complete the steps in this chapter
- [ ] Word count is 1200–2000 (longer needs a reason)

Reader-test checks (before this chapter ships with the book):

- [ ] At least one beta reader has followed the chapter from start to finish
- [ ] Where they got stuck is recorded in the beta reader log below
- [ ] The chapter has been revised based on what they got stuck on

## Beta reader log for this chapter

After the first draft is complete, send the chapter to at least one person and watch them try to do what it says. Log what you see.

```
Reader: [name]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Where they got stuck: [specific step or sentence]
What they said out loud at the stuck point: [their exact words]
What I changed in response: [specific edit]
```

Repeat for each beta reader until two readers in a row complete the chapter without getting stuck.

## Style rules

- Short declarative sentences.
- Real specifics, not hypotheticals. "Your first loaf will have a slightly pale top, because you didn't preheat the dutch oven" beats "your results may vary."
- Admit what's hard. If a step took you an hour the first time you did it, say so.
- Don't balance for balance. If one option is better, say so.
- Never pad to hit word count. If a chapter delivers its outcome in 900 words, it's a 900-word chapter.

## Banned phrases

"I'd be happy to." "Great question." "Let me help you with that." "Certainly." "Delve." "In today's fast-paced." "Unlock." "Leverage" (as a verb). "Empower." "At the end of the day." "The reality is." "It's worth noting that." "In conclusion." "Journey" (as a metaphor). "Ecosystem." "Seamless." "Robust." "Elegant solution." "Revolutionary." "Game-changing."

## No fabricated anecdotes

If a chapter benefits from a real story, pull it from interviews or research, or ask the author. Never invent. A fabricated anecdote will be obvious to one reader and will break trust with all of them.

## What this template is not

This template is not a container for insight essays. It is not a place to build up to a revelation. It is not a vehicle for the author's voice to shine (voice shines in sentence construction, not in structural cleverness).

It is a recipe. Each chapter is a recipe. The reader opens the book hungry and closes it having eaten.
