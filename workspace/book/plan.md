# Book Plan

**This is an example plan**, written to demonstrate what a good plan looks like. Replace every word below with your own book before you start writing. The writer agent reads this file as source of truth for what comes next, so don't leave the example in place unless you are literally writing a bread book.

---

## Title

How to Start Baking Bread at Home

## Subtitle

A small book for people who want a real loaf on the counter by Saturday afternoon.

## Premise

This is a book for the person who has three half-open bags of flour in the cupboard, an oven they mostly use to reheat pizza, and a quiet feeling that a homemade loaf would be nice if it weren't so complicated.

It isn't complicated. It has rules. The rules are not hard. The rules are just not what most recipes tell you.

I'm writing this as someone who ruined twenty loaves before getting one right. I'm not a baker by trade. I don't own a proofing cabinet. I use a $12 digital scale and an enamel dutch oven my mother gave me when I moved out.

Four rules for reading:

1. You don't need to know anything about fermentation. If a sentence starts sounding chemical, I'll translate.
2. You don't need sourdough starter. Three chapters in, you can decide whether you want to go there.
3. You can skip around. Chapter 1 gets you a loaf today. Everything after that is depth.
4. Every technique in this book has been used to make a loaf I actually ate.

## Audience

- Someone who can boil water but has never baked.
- Someone who has tried sourdough once, produced a brick, and given up.
- Someone who wants a low-stakes weekend project, not a new identity.

Not for professional bakers. Not for people who already own a kitchen scale, a banneton, and a lame.

## Chapters

### Ch 1 — "Your First Loaf in Four Hours"

The payoff: a loaf cooling on the counter before dinner.

Four-ingredient no-knead bread. Dutch oven. One rise. One bake. No starter. No shaping. Reader finishes the chapter eating bread.

### Ch 2 — "Why Your Dough Sticks to Everything"

The payoff: the reader understands hydration and stops fighting the dough.

What hydration means in one line. Why wet dough feels wrong but behaves right. The flour-hand-dough triangle. Two small fixes that change everything.

### Ch 3 — "The Pantry That Makes Everything Easier"

The payoff: a shopping list that fits in a pocket.

Flour types in plain English. Instant yeast versus active dry. Why a kitchen scale is the one tool that matters. What you don't need (banneton, lame, dutch oven replacement, sourdough starter).

### Ch 4 — "Knead Like You Mean It"

The payoff: the reader knows when the dough is done.

Hand kneading versus stand mixer. The windowpane test, demonstrated. The "it looks wrong, keep going" moment. When to stop.

### Ch 5 — "Time Is Your Secret Ingredient"

The payoff: a loaf with flavor people ask about.

Cold fermentation in plain English. The 24-hour fridge rise. Why supermarket bread tastes like nothing. How to plan a loaf around a workday.

### Ch 6 — "Reading Dough With Your Hands"

The payoff: the reader can tell when to bake without a timer.

The poke test. The jiggle test. Temperature. Why the clock is lying and the dough is telling the truth. One page of photos: overproofed, underproofed, just right.

### Ch 7 — "The Oven Dance"

The payoff: a crust that crackles when you cut it.

Steam, scoring, and temperature curves. Preheating the dutch oven. The first ten minutes versus the last twenty. What oven spring looks like. How to tell when a loaf is done by sound, not timer.

### Ch 8 — "When Things Go Wrong"

The payoff: a troubleshooting map the reader can hang on the fridge.

Dense. Gummy. Flat. Pale. Burned bottom. Big holes. No holes. Each problem gets one cause and one fix. No philosophy.

### Ch 9 — "Beyond Plain Bread"

The payoff: three recipes that reuse everything learned.

Focaccia. Enriched dough (for milk bread or dinner rolls). Introduction to sourdough with a shortcut starter. This is the "now you can leave the nest" chapter.

### Ch 10 — "Making It a Habit"

The payoff: a weekly rhythm that makes bread a non-event.

Saturday morning mixing. Sunday bake. Freezing loaves. Giving loaves away. How to stop feeling like every bake is a performance. The shift from "I made bread today" to "I bake bread."

## Notes for the writer

- Voice references: the plainness of Mark Bittman, the patience of Samin Nosrat, the no-nonsense of Kenji López-Alt.
- Tone: warm, direct, slightly embarrassed about earlier failures.
- Every chapter must have a real recipe or a real action. No essays.
- The reader finishes the book able to bake. Not inspired to bake. Able to bake.
- Don't write a chapter about "the history of bread." Bread history is fascinating, and no reader needs it to make a loaf tonight.
